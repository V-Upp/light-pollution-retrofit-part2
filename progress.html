Earlier — Problem framing

Brainstorming: why streetlights, and why a hood

Before settling on a retrofit hood, we worked through several other directions and ruled most of them out. Kept here mostly unedited, because the reasoning matters as much as the conclusion.

"Just turn off the lights?" — No. Lights are necessary for driver safety at night, so removal isn't a real option.
What are the actual requirements streetlighting has to meet? Are there things in DFW lighting that are unnecessary and contributing to light pollution? What are the main contributors?
If replacing streetlights outright, the bigger question is cost and installation effort — any fix needs to be cheap and easy enough that someone would actually adopt it.
Who owns and manages streetlights in the area? Utilities and municipal departments — companies like Oncor, along with city departments of transportation and public works. Oncor is already replacing older streetlights with LEDs, which is a separate but related trend.
The common fixture type across DFW is the cobra-head streetlight. What makes a streetlight bad for light pollution, and how could it be improved? Light shining sideways, and sometimes upward, is largely unnecessary and is a direct source of light pollution.
Idea: put a hood or cover on fixtures that are contributing to light pollution, to physically block the unnecessary light. This became the direction we pursued.

Cover / hood idea — early concerns

What other problems do hoods or shielded fixtures introduce? Blue-white light at high color temperatures can be more harmful/disruptive than warmer light — worth considering a filter, not just a shield.
Additional idea: add a light filter (this became the amber filter panel in the current CAD model).
Potential problems flagged early: overheating inside an enclosed hood, and the risk of failing roadway lighting safety requirements if too much light gets blocked. Both are still open questions — see Research.

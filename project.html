Project

Background & objective

Why this project exists, what we're trying to prove or disprove, and the constraints the design has to work within.

Background

In the winter of 2025, a road trip to Big Bend National Park — a designated International Dark Sky Sanctuary — changed how we thought about the night sky. Sitting by a campfire, we waited for the flames to die out completely so we could see true darkness.

The transition was striking. With no local light interference, the sky filled with a density of stars we hadn't seen before — some faint, some bright, some seeming to trace their own shapes. We could pick out Sagittarius, Capricornus, and plenty of unnamed patterns in between. For the first time, we saw the night sky at something close to its actual scale.

Returning to North Texas, the contrast was jarring. The clarity of West Texas was replaced by a washed-out urban skyglow. Constellations we'd mapped easily at Big Bend were gone, obscured by artificial twilight. That contrast raised an obvious question: why are we losing our night skies at home?

Our early research pointed to one contributor among several: standard, unshielded cobra-head streetlights, which are common across the Dallas–Fort Worth area and can send a meaningful share of their output sideways or upward instead of down onto the road. This notebook documents our investigation into a mechanical, retrofit-based approach to that problem.

The problem

An estimated 7 million DFW residents have limited or no view of the Milky Way

That figure reflects the approximate population of the Dallas–Fort Worth metro area, which sits under persistent urban sky glow. We treat it as context for why this problem is worth studying locally, not as a measurement we've produced ourselves.

Broader goal

A retrofit approach for common streetlights in a heavily lit urban area

Specifically: investigate whether a retrofit can reduce light pollution from common cobra-head streetlights already installed across Dallas–Fort Worth, without replacing them.

Objective

What we're testing for

Investigate whether a clamp-on retrofit hood can reduce glare and spill light without:

No drilling

Fixture stays mechanically unmodified.

No fixture replacement

Attaches to what's already installed.

No added wind risk

Shouldn't meaningfully increase wind loading on the pole.

No maintenance conflict

Doesn't block the access maintenance crews already need.

No safety compromise

Roadway illumination has to stay within acceptable range.

Low cost, low weight

Has to be realistic to manufacture and install at scale.

Engineering constraints

Design constraints

ConstraintReasoning
No drilling or permanent modificationPreserves the existing fixture and avoids warranty or liability issues for the utility.
Low added weightMinimizes additional structural load on the arm and pole.
Easy installation and removalNeeds to be practical for a maintenance crew, not just a lab bench.
Compatible with common cobra-head fixturesTargets standard tenon-mount hardware already in the field.
Minimal impact on maintenance accessShouldn't block the panels or points a crew already services.
Preserve roadway visibility and safetyThe retrofit cannot come at the cost of driver safety — this is the non-negotiable constraint.
Design philosophy

Every decision balances four factors

Lighting performance

Does it actually reduce glare and spill without gutting usable roadway light?

Manufacturability

Can it be built from common materials without specialized tooling?

Cost & maintenance

Would a utility or municipality find installation and upkeep worth it?

Safety underlies all three — it isn't a fourth variable to trade off, it's the constraint the other three operate inside of.

Known limitations

Being upfront about what this project has not yet done:

  • No prototype built yet
  • No thermal analysis performed
  • No photometric simulation run
  • No wind testing conducted
  • No structural validation completed
  • CAD model only, at this stage

Success criteria

We'll consider this design promising if future analysis shows:

  • Reduced glare
  • Reduced spill light
  • Acceptable roadway illumination
  • Manageable wind loading
  • Practical installation
  • Acceptable manufacturing cost

If analysis says otherwise, the design gets revised — or dropped.
